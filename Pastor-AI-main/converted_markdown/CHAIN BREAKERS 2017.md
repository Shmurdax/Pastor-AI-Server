## I. THE HEART OF THE SOWER...

I Pastored for a number of years in a farming community, and the lessons
I learned from those farmers has greatly enhanced my ability and
willingness to give to the Lord. I have yet to meet a farmer who sows
his fields without expecting the ground to produce a return. I disagree
with people who say we should not sow with an expectation to receive.
That is absolutely foolishness. Every farmer expects an increase in due
season. The farmer who sows without expecting to receive will be out of
business in short order.

Thought the farmer plants with an expectation of reaping, a true farmer
makes his living from the ground because he has a love for farming:
(PLACE ON THE SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- He maintains his equipment... During the winter the true farmer does
 not spend his days recklessly, he is in the shop getting the equipment
 ready for the next farming cycle.

- He plows the ground... He is preparing it to receive his seed.

- He Plants the crop... He understands that unless he makes an
 investment in sowing there will be no harvest.

- He tends the crop... He fertilizes, plows, sprays pesticides, etc...

- He reaps his reward... At the right time he goes into the field and
 gathers the harvest into the silo.

This is the pattern that is repeated year after year, however if the
farmer gets hailed out; flooded out; stormed out; droughted-out, he is
still there the next season because his heart is to farm, he has a
connection to the land. The smell of the soil gets him going in the
morning! The sound of the equipment working causes the blood to flow
properly through his veins! The sound of the combine thrashing the
harvest causes his heart to beat a little faster.

The law of reciprocity is a theme which is in vogue in the church
worldwide. I believe in the law of reciprocity and it is really the law
of the harvest. I take exception with much of the teaching I have heard
concerning this subject because it has as its foundation giving for the
purpose of receiving. This should never be our purpose. Our purpose for
giving should be two fold: (PLACE ON THE SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- A heart which is obedient to the will of the Father...

- A heart which desires to distribute resources according to the
 purposes of the Father...

A farmer farms because of the love of the land, yet receives the
benefits (more than he sowed) of his labor. The Christ honoring giver
gives because his heart demands it, yet he enters the blessed life
because he gave. The give and it shall be given principle is to be our
reward not our motive!

Let me share an often quoted, misunderstood scripture. Luke 6:38 (NLT)
"If you give, you will receive. Your gift will return to you in full
measure, pressed down, shaken together to make room for more, and
running over. Whatever measure you use in giving---large or small---it
will be used to measure what is given back to you." Most people would
say, if you give you will get more than you give. This is only half of
the truth being communicated here. The motivation should be on the
obedience of giving and the reward will take care of itself. In order to
properly understand this scripture, I want to look at the larger text.
Luke 6:35-40 (NLT) "[Love your enemies! Do good to them! Lend to
them! And don't be concerned that they might not repay. Then your reward
from heaven will be very great]{.underline}, and you will truly be
acting as children of the Most High, for he is kind to the unthankful
and to those who are wicked. 36 You must be compassionate, just as your
Father is compassionate. 37 "[Stop judging others, and you will not be
judged]{.underline}. [Stop criticizing others, or it will all come back
on you]{.underline}. [If you forgive others, you will be
forgiven]{.underline}. 38 [If you give, you will receive]{.underline}.
[Your gift will return to you in full measure, pressed down, shaken
together to make room for more, and running over]{.underline}. [Whatever
measure you use in giving---large or small---it will be used to measure
what is given back to you]{.underline}."

This scripture does talk about money but it doesn't address money
exclusively. In six short verses Jesus preaches a seven point sermon:
(PLACE ON THE SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- "Love your enemies! Do good to them! Lend to them! And don't be
 concerned that they might not repay. Then your reward from heaven will
 be very great." - If you will lend to those who have a need without
 forethought of repayment, heaven will stand good for the debt... How
 can this be true? This is because we are stewards and not owners... We
 are only managing the resources which belong to the Father... (We will
 talk about this later...) (My experience with a good friend who owed
 me \$120,000.00) (When we turn it over to God, they no longer owe us
 money, they owe God the money and He is pretty good at collecting old
 debts!)

- "Stop judging others, and you will not be judged." - If we sow
 compassion we will reap compassion...

- "Stop criticizing others, or it will all come back on you." - If
 we sow kindness we will reap kindness...

- "If you forgive others, you will be forgiven." - If we sow mercy
 we will reap mercy...

- "If you give, you will receive."- This scripture has been
 applied to money almost exclusively and it certainly embodies that
 aspect but it is a much broader statement which encompasses our
 actions, attitudes, and motives. He is saying that we should direct
 our lives with sowing and reaping at the forefront of our thought
 process because whatever we sow we are going to reap.

- "Your gift will return to you in full measure, pressed down, shaken
 together to make room for more, and running over." - The terms
 "full measure, pressed down, shaken together, and running over"
 were farming terms. In the Old Testament farmers were instructed to
 leave some grain in the corners of their fields for the poor to
 gather. Each year at harvest-time there were two sets of harvesters in
 the field, the primary harvesters which worked the middle of the field
 were actually hired by the landowner to help bring in the harvest; and
 the poor people in the corners of the fields which were harvesting
 grain to sustain their families. The harvesters in the middle of the
 field would fill their baskets and walk to the barn or wagon and empty
 the grain and go back to the middle of the field to repeat the
 process. Because they were being paid by the hour it did not matter to
 this group of harvesters how full their basket was, they were working
 until the harvest was reaped. They just needed to stay busy until the
 job was done. The next set of harvesters had a completely different
 motivation, they were poor and had probably traveled a great distance
 to be in the field and all they would be able to bring home for their
 families is what they could get in their basket! The contents of the
 basket were a matter of life and death to them! Because this was a
 matter of survival, the poor would not just get a half basket and
 leave the field, (ILLUSTRATE THIS) they would press-it-down,
 shake-it-together, and heap-it-up.

- "Whatever measure you use in giving---large or small---it will be
 used to measure what is given back to you." These are the words of
 Jesus not a television preacher. Remember, this is not exclusively
 about money, He is speaking of loving, loaning, having compassion,
 dispensing kindness, providing mercy, and giving money.

When a farmer sows he always receives more than he started with. Even in
the year when he is purported to have a complete crop failure he gets
more than he gives. While giving is our motivation rather than getting
the law of the harvest has been pre-determined, we will get more than we
give.

pure in their own eyes, but [the [Lord]{.smallcaps} examines their

expectantly await our harvest, our motivation for sowing should not
necessarily be reaping but giving and sharing. If our motivation for
sowing is reaping alone, if we do not reap what we expected our giving
comes to a screeching halt! If the farmer is in the farming business
strictly for the harvest, he would quit the first year he made a bad
crop!

Many Christians never discover the secret of joyous giving or the
blessing of sharing. The Word plainly say's the Father has uniquely
designed us to experience the joy of giving and the blessing of sharing.
Acts 20:35 (NLT) "And I have been a constant example of how you can
help the poor by working hard. You should remember the words of the Lord
Jesus: '[It is more blessed to give than to receive]{.underline}." If
we do not work we will not eat! 1 Thessalonians 3:10... The properly
motivated Christian is far more excited about giving than getting!

When a person begins to experience the exuberance of this kind of giving
it is much easier for them to enter this sphere in the future. The
condition of the heart determines whether a person is capable of this
kind of giving. Matthew 6:20-21 (NLT) "[Store your treasures in
heaven]{.underline}, where they will never become moth-eaten or rusty

we have belongs to God; we are the steward (Business Managers) of His

we view the possessions we have been blessed with as our own we tend to:

## III. THE REWARD OF THE SOWER...

When the farmer takes the bob trucks and combines into the field, he
knows that months of work are going to be rewarded with a harvest. The
resources the farmer receives from months of labor will be: (PLACE ON
THE SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- Tallied... He will total up his increase.

- Taken to the market... He will sell his harvest for as much as
 possible.

- Distributed... He will lay aside enough money to sow next year's crop
 and then everyone he owes will get their share.

- Budgeted... He will determine his bills and living expenses until the
 next harvest.

The heart of God is to make stewards of us so He can direct His
resources, and when this occurs we experience the completion of the
cycle of giving, which is receiving, so we can give again. Luke 6:38
(NLT) "[If you give, you will receive]{.underline}. Your gift will
return to you in [full measure, pressed down, shaken together to make
room for more, and running over]{.underline}. [Whatever measure you use
in giving---large or small---it will be used to measure what is given
back to you]{.underline}."

Once a person develops this kind of a heart, they are no longer a
reservoir which threatens to run dry, they become a river which has many
tributaries and no matter how great the outflow, the input just seems to
keep pace. When a person is a reservoir he is controlled by what he has
and grieved by what he gives, because he continually measures his
success and effectiveness by what he has accumulated.

When we have the heart of God and truly have become a steward we measure
our success by what we have given rather than by what we have received
or kept. Giving truly becomes an investment in His kingdom. Investing in
His kingdom produces eternal results.

The reward of the true giver is not the harvest, but the opportunity to
sow.

INTRO: I am preaching this morning on a very familiar subject -- The
Passover. Webster says Passover is "A Jewish holiday of seven days
duration, beginning on the fourteenth day of Nisan, and commemorating
the ancient Hebrews deliverance from Egypt."

As we all know, the Passover has its roots in early Old Testament times.
The first Passover had to do with God's deliverance of the Israelites
from the bondage that the Egyptians had brought them under.

The Jews were in bondage to the Egyptians and God spoke to a Hebrew man
by the name of Moses, when he was eighty years old (He had been drawing
Social Security for 15 years!) and told him that they were to kill a
sacrifice and place the blood of that innocent sacrifice on the door
posts and the lentil of their homes. Exodus 12:13 (NKJV)"Now the
blood shall be a sign for you on the houses where you are. And [when I
see the blood, I will pass over you]{.underline}; and the plague shall
not be on you to destroy you when I strike the land of Egypt."

The plague spoken of here was that the Death Angel would pass through
the land and any home not having the blood applied would experience the
Grim Reaper first hand through the death of the first born. (PLAY
VIDEO CLIP HERE)

This plague was to affect both man and beast. In this very first
Passover, there was a difference made between the Hebrews and the
Egyptians:

- The Hebrew's were about to have a feast; the Egyptians were going to
 have a funeral!

- The Hebrew's were going to experience deliverance; the Egyptians were
 about to embark upon a time of national mourning!

- The Hebrew's were about to experience Gods sustaining power; the
 Egyptians were going to experience the wrath of God!

- The Hebrew's were going to experience the provision of God; the
 Egyptians were about to experience the punishment of God!

Talk about role reversals! This is probably one of the most prolific
role reversals in the history of the world. The difference that was made
here was not a difference of:

- Race

- Nationality

- Economics

- Social standing

The difference between the Jews and the Egyptians was that the Jews had
applied the blood and the Egyptians had not.

## III. COMMUNION SHOULD BE A PRIVATE ACT OF PRAISE TO GOD

Don't you know that following that first Passover when the Egyptians had
given the Israelites all of their personal possessions and a great deal
of their national wealth and Pharaoh issued the edict that the
Israelites were not only free to leave Egypt but were being mandated to
do so, that there was a shout of praise that went up to God that day?

Can you just imagine the scene inside households as parents realized
their families had been spared the death penalty? The scene probably
spilled out into neighborhoods and city streets all over the land of
Goshen! I promise you, there was a lot of praising going on in Goshen on

There are two times to praise God:

- When we feel like it

- When we do not feel like it!

Hebrews 13:15 (NKJV)"Therefore by Him [let us continually offer the
sacrifice of praise to God]{.underline}, that is, the fruit of our lips,
giving thanks to His name."

Why should we praise him in the face of loss? Psalms 22:3 (NKJV) "But
You are holy, [Enthroned in the praises of Israel]{.underline}."

If Jesus could offer thanks to God over that which represented his
broken body and shed blood, surely we can praise God in spite of our
small problems.

As we receive communion this morning, let us not forget to:

- Make a new pledge to keep His memory alive

- Preview what is to come

- Praise God in spite of our problems
