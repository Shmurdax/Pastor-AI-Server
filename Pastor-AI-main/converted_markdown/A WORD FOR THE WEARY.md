# A WORD FOR THE WEARY

(The Gathering 2023)

All of us grow weary -- All those engaged in a God Vision suffer from
fatigue from time to time. (Joke about illness and fatigue -- Sick and
tired of going to work)

God knew that we would struggle with this emotion, which is why He tells
us, "Be not weary in well doing, for in DUE SEASON you shall REAP if
you do not FAINT."

We have all heard the old adage, "Rome wasn't built in a day." This is
so true, and nothing of significance gets built in a day. Please
remember, Rome didn't fall apart in a day either.

It is exhausting to think of all the attention it takes to detail, to
build something of significance. (Just building a house takes months and
includes thousands of details)

I am convinced that the weariness we suffer in ministry is not solely
the result of being overworked (Though we are at times), and living
under constant pressure (though we do -- Ministry is 24/7). It is the
result of:

- The spiritual battles we face...

- The physical challenges we encounter...

- The emotional toll that the journey generates...

- The relational trauma that can be inflicted along the way...

All of this to do what we believe to be the will of God. These
ingredients are a constant, in the crock pot, of what God is cooking.

I have a trivia question for you guys. Which building project took the
longest to complete? (PLACE ON THE SCREEN ONE AT A TIME AS I MENTION
THEM)

A. The construction of the Pentagon

B. The carving of Mount Rushmore

## C. The digging of the Panama Canal

D. The building of the Empire State Building

E. The carving and assembling of the Statue of Liberty

The answer is C - It took 31 years to dig the Panama Canal. The length
of this project was mainly because the project was started and stopped
several times due to floods, mudslides, and unexpected costs. The total
bill for the United States was \$375 million in 1914 (In today's economy
that would be almost 11 billion dollars).

The building of the Panama Canal resulted in the death of 20,000 French
workers and 6,000 American workers.

The moral of the story - When you build a 50 mile-long canal that
connects two oceans:

- Expect it to take longer than anticipated...

- Expect it to cost more than anticipated...

- Expect that some people who started with you not to be present when
 you finish...

The same is true of ministry -- We are not attempting to move millions
of tons of earth and connect two oceans, but the assignment He has
called us to, can be mind boggling -- emotionally draining -- and
absolutely exhausting.

If we don't feel overwhelmed at times by the task at hand, we are
probably focused on the wrong goal.

We can't accomplish a God vision without supernatural faith. (Fear is
the constant companion of all heroes of the faith)

We all love it when God gives us a promise---that's the easy part. Then
the grueling process begins. I am going to identify seven things that
must be a part of accomplishing all Kingdom projects: (Place on the
screen one at a time)

- Identifying the partners...

- Securing the resources...

- Developing the strategies...

- Casting the vision...

- Managing the process...

- Motivating the people...

- Waiting on the miracles...

Working with God is exhilarating when these things happen. But keeping
our faith at a level to accomplish the purpose of God is also warfare:
(Warfare = Weariness) Here are some constants along the way that lend
themselves to weariness:

- The obstacles...

- The waiting...

- The battles...

- The insults...

- The rejection...

- The disappointments...

- The casualties...

This process creates the desire to quit. We want to call in with illness
and fatigue.

Zerubbabel and Joshua, the two men commissioned to rebuild Solomon's
temple no doubt faced the phenomenon of weariness:

- The task was overwhelming...

- The cost was prohibitive...

- The workers were dismayed...

- The enemies were fierce...

These men of God struggled with intense discouragement as they looked at
the ruins of Jerusalem.

They started the work in earnest, but they heard a familiar voice that
whispered: "You'll never finish this. God is going to abandon you in
the middle of this project."

Fortunately, just when Zerubbabel and Joshua were about to throw in the
towel, the prophet Haggai showed up with a refreshing announcement.
"\'Now take courage, and work; for I am with you,\' declares the
Lord." Haggai 2:4 - The Lord also promised He would see the building
project to completion. "The latter glory of this house will be
greater than the former. And in this place, I will give peace."
Haggai 2:9

Those powerful prophetic promises propelled Zerubbabel and Joshua
forward. These words invigorated their weary faith and steeled their
determination. Their passion was refueled. They returned to the work,
even though it seemed impossible. In the end, God's house arose from an
ash heap.

This is God's promise to all who are called to labor with Him. He does
not tell you to begin something and then leave you halfway through it.

God is a wise builder and an expert craftsman. He is the Alpha and
Omega, the beginning and the end. He finishes what He starts. The
question is, are we going to finish what God has started?

CONCLUSION:

The apostle Paul spoke to weary workers when he wrote, "For I am
confident of this very thing, that He who began a good work in you will
perfect it until the day of Christ Jesus." Philippians 1:6 -- (Speak
this over each person in the room)

The Message Bible says it this way, "There has never been the
slightest doubt in my mind that the God who started this great work in
you would keep at it and bring it to a flourishing finish on the very
day Christ Jesus appears."

I want to encourage you with the words of Haggai - "Take courage! The
Lord is with you!"

Regardless of what you lack, the Lord's mighty presence is all you need
to finish the task. Hang on to Him and keep believing.
