but He discipled the disciples in small groups.

Jesus and the early church demonstrated for us the power and
effectiveness of Christian community. I believe, the church that
successfully adopts the New Testament pattern of community without
sacrificing its Pentecostalism will become powerful and effective force
for the last day's. (What would happen to the church if we were

In the Jesus church model the corporate gathering served a purpose and
was deemed very valuable. But notice, "He was moved with
compassion" because they were "Weary and scattered" because of
the lack of "Shepherds."

The bottom line is that Jesus taught and met the needs of the multitudes
but His long-range plan to care for them, was to provide them with
trained shepherds -- The Twelve Disciples.

Please know, the importance of the corporate gathering at CT will remain
in tact but we MUST find a way to provide a higher level of shepherding.
We believe that demands a strong life group ministry where people
shepherd (Disciple) people.

The fact is, Jesus created a Life Group to train those shepherds. Mark
3:13-14 (NKJV) "He went up on the mountain and called to Him those He
Himself wanted. And they came to Him. 14 Then He appointed twelve, that
they might be with Him and that He might send them out to preach."
Pay special attention to that point -- "That they might BE WITH
HIM."

Jesus' first meeting with the disciples was a home fellowship -- John
1:39 (NKJV) "They came and saw where He was staying, and remained
with Him that day ..."

In His first encounter with those who would become His disciples, Jesus
didn't take them to the Temple or the Synagogue to minister to them. He
brought them to His house.

Also, He used the "small group" concept to teach and mentor them . Mark
4:34 (NKJV)

"But without a parable He did not speak to them (crowds). And when
they were alone, He explained all things to His disciples."

According to Acts 2, the church of the first century (before the
persecution) met on regular basis in the temple and in the houses. After
the persecution and destruction of the temple they continued meeting but
they did so in houses. Because of this, the Christian community not only
survived, it thrived!

[Historical facts:]{.underline}

The people of Israel were divided into tribes, leaders of thousands,
hundreds, and tens in order to help them fulfill the law and take care
of the relational issues.

During the exile, the Israelites who were far from Jerusalem created the
synagogues. Synagogues were small centers of religious teaching, social
interactions and educational activities. The minimum number of members
was ten.

In Jesus' times, discipleship in small groups was a very common practice
among the Jewish and gentiles audiences. It was the way to develop
disciples and transfer the DNA from person-to-person and from
generation-to-generation.

Even when Jesus spent a lot of time with the multitudes, he spent more
time with the seventy and even more time with the twelve. Jesus preached
to the multitudes but to the seventy and especially the twelve, Jesus
explained His teachings in great detail, and took the time to deal with
their personal issues. The time He spent with the seventy and the twelve
increased significantly in the six months prior to his crucifixion.

The First Century Church received the instructions from Jesus to become
witnesses in Jerusalem, all of Judea, Samaria, and to the uttermost
parts of the earth. After they received the Holy Spirit they set up shop
in Jerusalem and stayed right there until the persecution started.
Staying in Jerusalem and having church was easy but it was not what they
had been instructed to do.

During this time there was addition but not multiplication. They even
had the need to implement structures in order to facilitate that growth
(Acts 6:1-7 Deacons). After the death of Stephen the Bible says "At
that time a great persecution arose against the church which was at
Jerusalem; and they were all scattered throughout the regions of Judea
and Samaria." It looks like they needed to be persecuted in order to
fulfill Jesus' instruction to go to the uttermost parts of the earth.

Beginning with the persecution, houses became the place where the
Christians had "church."

In the New Testament's vocabulary, the word "church" almost always
refers to the gathering of Christians in a house. There are twenty six
references in the New Testament that talk about believers meeting in
homes, or being part of a household.

In the year 70 the temple in Jerusalem was destroyed and it wasn't until
the year 150 when Christians were able to meet again in a place (not an
official temple) dedicated to worship. There was an eighty year stretch
where there was not a church building as we know them.

In the year 312, the Roman emperor Constantine (the first "Christian"
emperor) established Christianity as the official religion of the Empire
(Catholic Church). As a result of this he built temples and let the
Christians gather in public. Most of the Christians stopped meeting in
houses and started attending "church" where there was a "professional
minister" who was the only one authorized to teach the Bible.

Believe it or not, this ushered the Christian church into its darkest,
most powerless, ineffective era ever. The church lost the ministry of
the believers (Body of Christ) and became a religious/political
institution.

In 1500 Martin Luther (Protestant Reformation) brought the Bible back to
the Christians (in their own language) and confronted the corrupted
church, BUT he didn't change the "temple centered" paradigm.

In 1700 John Wesley was the first evangelical minister who organized the
new believers (disciples) into "classes" in order to help them walk out
their faith. John Wesley is the father of mall group ministry.

In our era the healthiest and growing churches have Small Groups as a
vital part of their structures. They are not churches with small groups;
they are small group churches.

Moving toward small groups is simply getting us back to the original
blueprint of history's most successful church -- The First Century
Church!

The church that organizes itself around the biblical principle of Life
Groups will have energized leaders and growing and energized members.

In Moses' previous model (Most churches operate in this model) there is
great potential for the leader to begin to lead poorly -- because of

The Biblical role of a pastor is for the pastor to:

- Pray and seek the face of God...

- Teach the truths of the Word of God...

- Receive revelation from God...

- Project the vision for the church...

- Equip the people to do everything else...

It is very important for the pastors and the people to embrace the
Biblical role of the pastor and the believers. Everyone stay in their
lane.

Ten reasons why life (small) groups are vital for a healthy-growing
church:

- Personal discovery... Personal discovery happens in small groups
 better than large groups for a number of reasons. You can learn, ask
 questions, involve yourself in the lives of others, and generally make
 yourself vulnerable among other people who are doing the same in small
 groups. You just can't do that in a corporate gathering designed for
 worship and preaching.

- Smaller communities are more effective... Smaller communities function
 more like, communities. That may seem like a given, but the bigger the
 group is, the less like community it feels. The kind of community I am
 advocating requires a level of intimacy easily lost as numbers grow.
 You simply cannot know everyone beyond a certain point, and you
 certainly will not open up about your struggles and sins in a large
 group of people you don't know.

- Deeper Friendships... Small groups deliver deeper friendships that
 double as accountability. When people know you, really know you, your
 life becomes far more transparent, including your sin. Others learn to
 read you and will call you out for those sins, creating opportunities
 to deal with real life difficulties as they surface. This is part of
 what we should expect from good friends. This provides a real feeling
 of being part of God´s family.

- Maximum participation... Small groups deliver maximum participation.
 There are opportunities to discuss the issues with others in the
 church. Life issues can be discussed openly among trusted friends.
 Mission can be planned out and participated in together. Lives are
 sharpened and leaders developed. Small groups are an absolute
 necessity for involving as many people as possible in the life and
 ministry of your church.

- Better understanding of the Bible... Preaching and teaching are
 one-way communication. People listen while the speaker speaks. This
 model is fine for imparting knowledge but not as effective for
 personal application as a small group. In a small group setting,
 people can ask questions, participate in a discussion of the text, and
 hear others share insights and illustrations of the truth you are
 trying to grasp. The Bible must be applied to one's own personal
 situations and that happens best in small groups.

- Provides a comfortable introduction for nonbelievers to the Christian
 faith... Inviting people to the church is, very often, intimidating to
 believers as well as for non- believers. Inviting someone instead to a
 small group meeting provides a non-threatening way for a believer to
 get a non-believer into a community of faith. It is here that the
 non-believer is brought face-to-face with the reality of the
 difference between the converted and unregenerate life. They begin to
 see believers live out their faith, pray for one another, share God's
 word, give testimony of God's work in their lives. Small groups are a
 powerful missional tool that allows for a more natural spread of the
 gospel among nonbelievers.

- Provides an ideal way to care for the needs of people within the
 church. When one believer in a small group is struggling financially,
 emotionally, spiritually, socially, etc., it is much easier for the
 members of the group to notice and provide help.

- Provide a way for Christians to live out their faith instead of merely
 hearing more preaching or teaching. If Sunday morning is for
 listening, then the rest of the week is for living. Whether it's
 discussing the Sunday sermon, talking about a spiritual battle, or
 simply praying for one another, small groups create a context for
 Christians to live out their faith in real life.

- Provides a source of encouragement and accountability. It's easy to
 slip in and out of church unnoticed. It's not just mega churches where
 this happens. In an average-sized church of 100 or 150, people may be
 coming to each Sunday service, but not getting involved. These people
 may need accountability in their lives, encouragement in their walk
 with God, or help in some way. Small groups provide a way to better
 meet these needs.

- Energy and synergy is created when people learn to care for each other
 on a more personal level

ministry. This is why He trained leaders (Twelve Disciples) and sent the

Small groups are the perfect place to develop, communicate and carry out
the three verbs of the CT vision:

- Disciple...

- Empower...

- Serve...

In the context of the larger church body we all have an area of
giftedness. Romans 12:6-8 (NKJV) "Having then gifts differing
according to the grace that is given to us, let us use them: if
prophecy, let us prophesy in proportion to our faith; 7 or ministry, let
us use it in our ministering; he who teaches, in teaching; 8 he who
exhorts, in exhortation; he who gives, with liberality; he who leads,
with diligence; he who shows mercy, with cheerfulness."

Each person's giftedness should be utilized and maximized. This is very
difficult to do in the corporate worship and instruction setting. When
the church is broken down into bite sized pieces more people can
contribute and utilize their gifts and the church is made healthier
because more gifts are functioning as we endeavor to disciple, and
empower each believer and we serve one another and the greater good of
our community.

The ease with which the church can expand through small groups is
exponentially better than the corporate model.

For the first-century Christians "church" meant "house." Perhaps one of
the reasons for the rapid advancement of the gospel was that the model
was not just a few clergymen preaching in pulpits, it was groups of
Christians growing and developing in their gifts together.

Christian community is not something new it is simply sharing a common
life in Christ. Life groups are intended to move us beyond the
self-interested isolation of our private lives and challenges us instead
to commit ourselves to do life together as the people of God.

We know all too well that maturity takes time. We know less well that it
also takes our sisters and brothers in Christ. It's a process that is
revealed in the "each other" language of the New Testament. Healthy
life groups will teach us to:

- Love one another...

- Forgive each other...

- Pray for each other...

- Serve each other...

- Submit to each other...

- Bear one another's burdens...

- Regard each other more highly...

- Teach and correct each other

- Become an encouragement to each other...

Christian Community (Common unity) is more easily developed in small
groups. Christian community is:

- A way to see Christ in others...

- A source of accountability and guidance...

- A place to pray and worship... (It will not be another church service)

- A place to serve...

- A witness to the world... (All men will know you have passed from
 death to life because you love one another)
