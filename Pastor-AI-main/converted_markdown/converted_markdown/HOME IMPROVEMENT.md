PROBLEM SOLVING

The old saying goes, "There are only two things you can be sure of, death and taxes." Whenever you live with others, there is another thing you can be sure of: There will be problems from time to time.

(Place on screen one at a time)

Be assured, Conflict is a normal part of marriage. That's not just your marriage, that's everyone's marriage. How we deal with conflict, can make or break our relationships.

Often our ability to handle conflict is stifled by our very fragile egos. Even well established ego's become bruised in the course of a long- term relationships.

A bruised ego often results in a closed spirit. A closed spirit behaves itself in the following ways: (Place on screen one at a time)

- Anger...

- Speaking harsh words...

- A breakdown in trust...

- Being rude in public or private...

- Unwillingness to admit wrongdoing...

- Taking ones spouse for granted...

- Making jokes or sarcastic comments at the expense of ones spouse...

- Forcing ones spouse into doing something they are not comfortable doing...

- Believing ones needs are more important than the needs of ones partner...

There are three very necessary ingredients in developing good problem solving skills: (Place on screen one at a time)

- Overcome the tendency to jockey for control... Control is never what you thought it would be. You may get what you wanted, but I promise, you won't want what you get!

- Be a good listener... Listening involves more than hearing. Listening involves shutting out the peripheral noise and focusing on what ones spouse is saying. Listening is being able to read between the lines -- hearing what is not being said is as important as what is being said.

- Being able to compromise... (Peacekeeper vs. Peacemaker) One should never give up core values, but much of what we are willing to die for has nothing to do with core values. Everyone needs to be able to give a little here and there. (Flexibility is the key) This is not Burger King you can't have it your way every time!

There should be a healthier approach to disagreements in marriage than what most couples employ, and there is. This approach begins with the notion that no one will emerge from the other side of a discussion having achieved the total implementation of their ideas as opposed to the ideas of the other person.

If you come into a discussion prepared to give some ground or admit to the possibility that you are not 100% right, the major barrier has been removed.

Many times when there are disagreements they stem from a lack of self-confidence on the part of one or both parties in the relationship. In this case we should be attempting to build confidence in our partner who is lacking in this area.

We should lovingly explain how they are capable of working through the difficulty. We should make our love, concern and support apparent without overwhelming them with a self help paradigm.

When both participants in a discussion enter with the firm commitment that they will emerge victorious from the battle, it is highly unlikely that anything significant at all will be accomplished.

When we become better at problem solving than we are at standing our ground in an argument, we have the opportunity to open the closed spirit of our partner. How do we go about opening the spirit of our spouse? Sixteen rules to openness in a relationship: (Place on screen one at a time)

- Work toward the happiness of our spouses...

- Say kind things that affirm our spouses... Lovingly say what you mean but mean what you say.

- Value the opinion of our spouses...

- Offer trust before it is earned...

- Quickly admit when we are wrong...

- Do not embarrass our spouses in public...

- Place the comfort of ones spouse ahead of our own...

- Place the fulfillment of ones spouse ahead of our own...

- Attempt to win an agreement, not an argument...

- Attempt to hear what one is saying from the other person's point of view...

- Remember, if we lose your temper, we have lost our point...

- Spend as much time listening as we do making our case...

- Stay with the issue, don't drag in unrelated material and use it to devastate...

- Know how to tell the difference between facts and opinions... Opinions change, facts rarely change.

- Remember, victory by humiliation is no victory at all...

- Negotiate, don't exterminate... Proverbs 15:1 "A gentle answer turns away wrath, but a harsh word stirs up anger."

COMMUNICATION

The single most important factor in building deep interpersonal relationships in the family system is the presence of adequate skills in communication. When marriage or family systems function well, healthy and accurate communication is one of the most evident characteristics within such relationships.

Communication in marriage can be defined as the mutual exchange of thoughts and feelings between marriage partners. (Leave on screen until next slide) Please remember, just because you said it does not mean you have communicated it!

The process of communication involves the use of the entire body: (Place on screen one at a time)

- Our ears to hear... Really hear -- What is being said and what is not being said.

- Our voices to send messages... The right kind of messages -- Carefully chosen words -- In the right tones.

- Our hands... An open hand says we are open to discussion -- A closed hand says we are closed to discussion and represents anger.

- Our faces... Be careful about facial expressions -- A great exercise is to get in front of a mirror and practice saying what you want to say and watch your facial expressions.

Our body language in general add to the messages we are attempting to send or close the other person off to the message we want to send. A tear rolling down the cheek speaks powerfully, as does the quivering hand or the rolling eyes.

Becoming an effective communicator involves being sensitive to how we use our body in the process, and how others are adding to the meaning of messages by the use of theirs. As we attempt to hone our communication skills we must be constantly aware of the tone of our voice as well as the degree of our attentiveness.

When we are attempting to share our feelings with our spouse, we will be far more successful if we use "I" messages rather than "you" messages. "You make me feel" sounds like an accusation. "I feel" is a much better method of sharing our feelings.

Attitude is perhaps the most important ingredient to successful communication. (Leave on screen until next slide) Statements like "You shouldn't feel that way," or "How could you possibly feel that way," will most likely shut the other person down right away! A better way of addressing the issue is "Why do you feel that way?" or "What have I done to cause you to feel that way?"

This attitude will help our spouses feel free to share a problem with us in a way that will allow for a solution rather than a stalemate.

Communication is an active, dynamic, and skilled process. (Leave on screen until next slide) It is a process that we can become very good at if we are committed to growth in this area.
