# MIRACLES, SIGNS, AND WONDERS

TEXT: Acts 2:22--24 (NKJV) "Men of Israel, hear these words: Jesus of Nazareth, a Man attested by God to you by miracles, wonders, and signs which God did through Him in your midst, as you yourselves also know--- 23 Him, being delivered by the determined purpose and foreknowledge of God, you have taken by lawless hands, have crucified, and put to death; 24 whom God raised up, having loosed the pains of death, because it was not possible that He should be held by it." 

INTRO: Our text is an excerpt from the first Evangelistic/Pentecostal message ever preached. It is here that the Apostle Peter introduced Jesus of Nazareth as the Son of God, the Messiah, with the words, "a Man attested by God to you by miracles, wonders, and signs which God did through Him in your midst, as you yourselves also know."

The Greek word translated "attest" here, means "to cause something to be known as genuine, to make clearly known."

Jesus ministered to the people for three and a half years, teaching them about God, thus proclaiming the Good News. But it was the miracles, signs, and wonders that proved who He was, and that His message was genuine.

The message was not the miracles, signs, and wonders, but they were the things God employed to prove the genuineness of Jesus' message, and to confirm that He was in fact the One they had been waiting for. The miracles, signs, and wonders proved Him to be the Son of God, the Messiah, their Savior.

The proof that we are who we say we are, and the proof that our message is true is miracles, signs, and wonders. "These signs SHALL follow those who believe..."

DEFINITION OF MIRACLES, SIGNS AND WONDERS

These three words, "Miracles, Signs and Wonders" are frequently found together in the New Testament in one order or another.

"Miracles, Signs, and Wonders" speak about three different aspects of the nature and power of God. While the function of these three words are complementary, together they bring about the full impact of who God is, and what He does.

1) The Greek word translated as "miracles" is (δύναμις) dunamis, and it means "a deed manifesting great power with the implication of some supernatural force." Oftentimes we find it translated in the New Testament as "power, mighty works, and strength."

2) The Greek word translated as "sign" is (σημεῖον) semeion, and it means "a sign, a mark; by which a person or thing is distinguished from others and is known; an event which is regarded as having some special meaning; not simply a miraculous event but something which points to a reality of even greater significance."

3) The Greek word translated as "wonder" is (τέρας) teras, and it means "a marvel." It speaks to the wonder or awe that is produced in those who witness the miracle, or the dunamis.

Therefore:

- A miracle is the supernatural power of God being manifest in the natural realm...

- A sign points up the fact that no one but God could have done such a thing... (Put up sign -- "I did this" God)

- A wonder is the awe that is produced in the hearts of the people as they witness the miracle...

All three together, miracles, signs, and wonders give a complete revelation of the supernatural power of God and are utilized in turning our hearts, and the hearts of others to God.

(The Joshua and Hunter Paul story -- The miracle is that they were shot 11 times and lived -- The sign is that no one but God could have done such a thing -- The wonder is the awe that is produced when people hear the story!)

(Story of Tanaya in India -- The miracle is that God raised a child to life -- The sign is that no one but God could have done such a thing -- The wonder is that the hearts of the people were turned to God.)

THE GENUINE

Acts, Chapter 8, provides a clear and stark contrast between the signs produced by the Holy Spirit and those produced by Satan.

Simon the sorcerer had been performing miracles for a long time in Samaria. In so doing, he had convinced all the people that he was operating in "the great power of God." Then Philip came to this same city, and began to preach the Gospel and performed miracles. He healed the sick and cast out demons.

 

What is noteworthy in this story is that once Philip arrived and began to minister by the power of the Holy Spirit and preach the Gospel, the entire population of the city, including Simon, believed his message.

Both Simon and Philip performed miracles, so how was it that no one questioned which of the two was right? Everyone immediately knew they had been deceived by Simon's miracles and that Philip's message, ministry, and miracles were true.

The key to distinguishing between one operating by the power of the Holy Spirit, and one operating by demonic powers, is found in the signs. "He (Simon) had astonished them with his sorceries for a long time" -- The only thing Simon's ministry produced was that the people were astonished by the miracles. They had no other effect. (The devil can do supernatural things -- Only God can bless us -- The devil cannot curse what God has blessed)

In contrast to Simon's ministry, the signs of Philip's ministry were "unclean spirits, crying with a loud voice, coming out of many who were possessed; and many who were paralyzed and lame, being healed," and "there was great joy in that city."

Philip's ministry produced freedom, changed lives, and brought great joy. Simon's actions did not change lives for the better, but produced fear and uncertainty, as the people looked on at his miracles. As the King James translation puts it, "He bewitched the people."

Those involved in the occult, witchcraft, horoscopes, séances, ouija boards, and other demonic practices, who try to find out about, or manipulate the future, experience fear, foreboding, and uncertainty.

In contrast, those led by the Holy Spirit and the gifts of the Spirit, experience wonderful peace, confidence, joy, and an unshakeable assurance in the one and only true God, who alone holds the future.
