## II. INTEGRITY DEVELOPMENT

Genesis 39:1-6 (NKJV) "Now Joseph had been taken down to Egypt. And Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh, captain of the guard, an Egyptian, bought him from the Ishmaelites who had taken him down there. The Lord was with Joseph, and he was a successful man; and he was in the house of his master the Egyptian. By the age of seventeen or so, Joseph has endured more hardship than most people encounter in a lifetime. Joseph had already faced: (PLACE ON SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- Rejection of his dream

- Rejection of his honesty

- Rejection from his brethren

Rejection brings with it one of the greatest emotional battles one can fight. In the midst of the process of dealing with the rejection of his brothers, Joseph finds himself being sold into slavery to an Egyptian businessman.

Think of this: the dream was given by God and rather than moving toward the pinnacle of success, Joseph is thrown away by his brothers, left for dead, and twice sold.

This thing is not turning out like Joseph had imagined. I don't think I have ever talked to a successful person who has not had their fair share of pain and rejection.

Joseph settles into life as a slave serving his master Potiphar. As he serves Potiphar, God begins to bless Potiphar in unusual ways. The blessing of God upon Joseph's work catches the attention of Potiphar. Being the good businessman he is, Potiphar slowly begins to give Joseph extra responsibility as well as authority. Joseph garners more responsibility and authority in Potiphar's house than one would think a slave would ordinarily be able to acquire.

What we do with authority and responsibility will be a factor in whether or not we will be able to achieve our destiny. A little authority goes a long way with some people; others view authority as an opportunity to faithfully discharge responsibility. Authority allows for some people to become dictators; others allow authority to hone their leadership skills and actually serve in a more beneficial and effective way.

Integrity serves as a voltage regulator for authority!

Joseph was one of those leaders who exercised wisdom when given authority. As a result, added authority and responsibility pointed out the fact that Joseph not only could be trusted but should be trusted. George Bernard Shaw said, "We are made wise not by the recollection of our past, but by the responsibility for our future." (LEAVE ON SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE)

In the process of time, Potiphar gives Joseph such responsibility and authority that Joseph is made the Business Manager for Potiphar.

Joseph scored pretty high when confronted with difficulty, but how would he fare in the face of success? As with Joseph, we all face two very vulnerable moments in one's life: (PLACE ON SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- When we face trials

- When we achieve success

In the midst of the greatest level of success Joseph had known in his short life, he was about to be confronted with the greatest test to date. This test would be a test of his integrity. Abraham Lincoln said (PLACE THIS QUOTE ON THE SCREEN FIRST THEN ADD THE NEXT QUOTE TO IT), "Nearly all men can stand adversity, but if you want to test a man\'s character, give him power." Warren Buffet said, "In looking for people to hire, you look for three qualities: integrity, intelligence, and energy. And if they don\'t have the first, the other two will kill you." (LEAVE BOTH QUOTES ON THE SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE)

Integrity and character are essential cornerstones which one should build any success upon. One cannot succeed over the long haul if this bedrock ingredient is missing from one's life.

Joseph was a young, virile man who was already fighting against the demands of testosterone when the wife of Potiphar begins an onslaught of seduction in relation to Joseph. She subjects him to this overwhelming attempt to get him to commit an immoral act with her day after day.

Joseph allows his integrity to be developed by continuing to reject the untoward advances of Mrs. Potiphar. Aristotle said, "Men acquire a particular quality by constantly acting in a particular way." (LEAVE ON SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE) This is exactly what Joseph did, day after day; he acted with integrity until he became a man of integrity!

A person does not become known as a person of integrity by making one good decision. Integrity often involves a lifetime of good choices. On the other hand, one can work a lifetime to build a reputation of character and integrity only to throw it all away with one bad decision. Tyron Edwards said, "Thoughts lead on to purposes; purposes go forth in action; actions form habits; habits decide character; and character fixes our destiny." (LEAVE ON SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE)

Joseph rejects the sexual advances of Mrs. Potiphar and even goes so far as to completely avoid being in her presence. It is easy to talk about becoming a person of integrity, but this involves a lifetime of good decisions and taking the high road in every situation.

The last time I checked, prison is not the most comfortable place in the world. Most criminals are not sorry for what they did, they are sorry they got caught. Their sorrow does not center around what they did wrong, but on the loss of freedom.

During Joseph's time in prison, the butler and baker of the king were incarcerated. Some time later, both the butler and baker dreamed dreams and Joseph interpreted their dreams with the stipulation that when the butler returned to the palace he would tell Pharaoh of Joseph's false imprisonment.

Just as Joseph predicted, the butler was restored to the king's favor and the baker was hanged. (He must have been a really bad cook!)

When the butler was restored to the king's favor, Joseph must have nervously awaited the call of the warden for Joseph to meet with the king. He probably anxiously anticipated the meeting with the king so he could plead his case and once-and-for-all prove his innocence.

Minutes turned into hours, hours turned into days, days turned into weeks, weeks turned into months, months turned into years -- two years to be exact. Joseph must have felt that his life would never be disconnected from the emotional roller coaster he seemed to be hopelessly trapped on.

After two years, Pharaoh dreams a dream which neither he nor his spiritual advisers could interpret. The butler remembers Joseph and the King calls for him. Joseph tells the king there will be seven years of bumper crops in Egypt followed by seven years of famine. Joseph advises the King to store grain and food in the seven years of plenty so that the Kingdom could be sustained during the seven years of famine.

Pharaoh agrees with the interpretation and makes Joseph the Prime Minister. Joseph is there. He has arrived. He finally made it. He is now doing the dream!

God almost never works within our time frame. We cannot afford to attempt to squeeze God into our time restraint but patiently await His timing!
