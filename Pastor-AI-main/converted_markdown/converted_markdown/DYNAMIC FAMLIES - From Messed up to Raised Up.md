INTRO: It's Resurrection Sunday! That is what Easter is all about. It is about a loving Heavenly Father so concerned about our sin-sickened state that He sent His Son, Jesus, to defeat sin and death through His cross and resurrection.

Just three days prior, Jesus had been falsely accused, tried and unfairly convicted. He was sentenced to the cruel death of the cross.

Shockingly, this was all God's pre-determined plan, His way to bring salvation to us.

Jesus willingly bore our sins. Imagine standing in a court, guilty and rightfully convicted, but then one stands up to voluntarily pay the fine and take our place. How would we feel toward such a man? That's Jesus!

Jesus not only took our punishment, He also demonstrated His power by taking up His life on the third day. He lives now to:

- Pray for us...

- Guide us...

- Heal us...

- Deliver us...

And He is coming again to gather us!

Resurrection is about that first Easter but it is about far more than that. Easter or resurrection should be on display all day, every day and in every way, for those who are Christ followers.

Over the next four weeks we are going to learn about a family that wandered away and suffered great loss, but through the redemptive plan of God experienced restoration and new life, may I say, resurrection life!

Ruth 1:1-6 (NKJV) "Now it came to pass, in the days when the judges ruled, that there was a famine in the land. And a certain man of Bethlehem, Judah, went to dwell in the country of Moab, he and his wife and his two sons. 2 The name of the man was Elimelech, the name of his wife was Naomi, and the names of his two sons were Mahlon and Chilion Ephrathites of Bethlehem, Judah. And they went to the country of Moab and remained there. 3 Then Elimelech, Naomi's husband, died; and she was left, and her two sons. 4 Now they took wives of the women of Moab: the name of the one was Orpah, and the name of the other Ruth. And they dwelt there about ten years. 5 Then both Mahlon and Chilion also died; so the woman survived her two sons and her husband."

This story takes place in the time of the Judges ... a period around 1,300 B.C. when various leaders, or judges, ruled in Israel.

First, we note that there was a famine in the land of Israel. Bethlehem means "The house of bread."

The famine became an external stressor that provoked an improper reaction from Elimelech. External stressors often have this effect. This is why it is so important to have a person of faith leading the household.

The improper reaction was to move to the land of Moab. Moab was the perennial enemy of Israel. They oppressed Israel for 18 years at a different point in time during the period of the Judges, and were well known for their worship of the god Chemosh. The followers of Chemosh practiced human sacrifice!

It is into this place -- into a worldly place -- that Elimelech sought refuge in his desperation. Can you imagine making a decision to take your family into such a place?

Most likely, Elimelech planned to stay in Moab only a short time. He had ancestral lands in Israel, a home, a social network and surely he meant not to permanently forsake those blessings and benefits. Gentlemen, the consequences of how we lead our families are going to eventually be laid at our feet.

The world (Moab) will always:

- Take us further than we intended to go...

- Keep us longer than we intended to stay...

- Extract from us far more than we intended to pay...

A family of four made the trip to Moab, Only one returned!

Moab became a place of death for the family of Elimilech. Upon her later return to Israel, Naomi declares, Ruth 1:21(NKJV) "I went out full, and the Lord has brought me home again empty." Remember they left Bethlehem because of a famine! Ten years of living in the world changed her perspective. I paraphrase this Scripture as follows "The worst day at church is better than the best day in the world."

Sometime after her husband and sons had died, Naomi heard while in Moab that, Ruth 1:6 (NKJV) "The Lord had visited His people (Israel) by giving them bread."

Though God's people may see seasons of leanness, God always visits in due time with His provision.

I'm going to skip over some details today, because over these next three Sundays we are going to learn from this book of Ruth how to carry ourselves as husbands, as wives, and how to see the next generation, our kids, restored.

While in Moab:

- Elimelech died...

- The two sons died...

- Naomi encouraged her daughters in law to remain in Moab while she returned to Bethlehem...

- Ruth, decided to accompany her mother in law to Bethlehem saying, "wherever you go I will go... Wherever you lodge I will lodge... Your people shall be my people... Wherever you die I will die..."

- Ruth went to work in the field of Boaz who was a near relative to Elimelech...

- Boaz noticed Ruth...

There existed a law in those days that if a man died, his nearest male relative was to marry the widow and through that union to produce heirs to continue the family name and inheritance of the land.

Ruth's diligence and devotion to her mother-in-law won her the respect and love of Boaz, who upon her request exercised this right of a near relative or "kinsman." He married Ruth and through this union restored the fortunes and the generations to the family of Naomi.

The book of Ruth closes with this, Ruth 4:21 (NKJV) "Boaz begat Obed; Obed begot Jesse, and Jesse begot David."

Boaz and Ruth became the great grandparents of David, Israel's second king and Jesus came to us through the line of David.

The author tells us that Boaz was a man of virtue and of wealth. He had the heart and he had the ability to redeem this messed up situation. And, he was a near relative. He had not only the ability and the desire, but he obtained the authority.

We have all lived in the land of Moab spiritually. We have wandered from God, living according to our own ways, and have introduced sin and dysfunction into our family lines.

The good news is that if we recognize the error of our ways and return to Bethlehem (Jesus) He possesses the ability to fix up our messed up situation.

Perhaps our younger families have not seen the full effects of remaining in Moab, but I promise you that our older couples, who have raised families, they would implore you today to leave that place and return home to the Lord before it's too late.

Generations are in the balance. The consequence of remaining in Moab is too severe to risk.

Can we really afford to remain in the land of Moab? Moab claims our children and our families. Naomi buried a husband and two sons in Moab!

Moab for us is the dominion of death. It's a place outside of God's blessing and of God's best. It's a place where Satan bears deadly influence and brings bondage, decay and loss.

Today I want to challenge the father's to take your family by the hand and make a decision to move from Moab to Bethlehem.

Boaz is a picture of our Lord Jesus Christ. Boaz had the willingness, the wealth and the authority to redeem this messed up situation.

Jesus took upon Himself human flesh -- He became a near relative to us! He had the willingness, He had the wealth, the ability and He had the authority to do so -- and He did!

Will you take your family by the hand today and make a decision to leave the messed up land of Moab and flee to the raised up land of Bethlehem.

The Word of God teaches us that the husband is the God-ordained head of the home. He is responsible for the provision, care and nurture of the family under his headship.

Elimelech, when faced with the challenges of famine, led his family to seek refuge in the land of Moab.

We discussed last week how Moab had been a perennial adversary of Israel and was known for its pagan worship. The results of his decision were disastrous.

As husbands, we bear the role of headship in the family. Headship does not mean "dictatorship." It means being responsible for the wellbeing of those under your care.

The meaning of the word responsibility according to Dictionary.com: The state or fact of being responsible, answerable, or accountable for something within one\'s power, control, or management.

The husband is in authority over the family. He is also accountable to God for what he did with what he has. Ephesians 5:22-33 (NKJV) "Wives, submit to your own husbands, as to the Lord. 23 For the husband is head of the wife, as also Christ is head of the church; and He is the Savior of the body. 24 Therefore, just as the church is subject to Christ, so let the wives be to their own husbands in everything.

25 Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ also loved the church and gave Himself for her, 26 that He might sanctify and cleanse her with the washing of water by the word, 27 that He might present her to Himself a glorious church, not having spot or wrinkle or any such thing, but that she should be holy and without blemish. 28 So husbands ought to love their own wives as their own bodies; he who loves his wife loves himself. 29 For no one ever hated his own flesh, but nourishes and cherishes it, just as the Lord does the church. 30 For we are members of His body, of His flesh and of His bones. 31 "For this reason a man shall leave his father and mother and be joined to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh." 32 This is a great mystery, but I speak concerning Christ and the church. 33 Nevertheless let each one of you in particular so love his own wife as himself, and let the wife see that she respects her husband." A Godly husband should focus on three things with regard to his wife and family:

- Love them... ("As Christ loved the church.") I am sure Elimelech loved Naomi but in his haste to take the course of least resistance he failed to "Give himself for her."

- Sanctify and cleanse them... (With the blood and the word) Elimelech probably adored his sons but he placed them in harms way in his irresponsible effort to make a living.

- Present them... Elimelech led his family into the teeth disaster. He became a failed father and husband. (We will one day stand before God and give account for how we led our families)

This idea of headship in no way carries with it the idea of individual superiority. No more than we think a teacher is superior to his students, but he or she is responsible for the conduct of that classroom and to work for the advancement of those pupils.

So also, a Christian husband and father is to work for the provision, orderliness, spirituality and advancement of his family.

If the story of Ruth and Naomi were to have ended at chapter one, what a sad and tragic tale it would have been, but the story does not end there!

Naomi heard, while in Moab, that God had visited Israel by giving them bread. After ten years of hell in Moab, she decided it was time to head home.

Ruth, with loving devotion toward Naomi, joined Naomi on her return to Israel and committed herself to become a worshiper of God.

Her attitude and actions caught the eye of Boaz, a wealthy man who owned a field wherein Ruth just so happened, by the providence of God, to work and glean. Ruth 2:1 (NKJV) "There was a relative of Naomi's husband, a man of great wealth, of the family of Elimelech. His name was Boaz."

Learning of Boaz' interest in Ruth, Naomi counseled Ruth so that she might seek his affections toward marriage.

For in those ancient times, it was the custom that a near male relative ought to marry the widow of his near relative and raise up children by her to continue the dead man's family line and ancestral inheritance.

Boaz, a wealthy near relative of Naomi took notice of Ruth's diligent work gleaning in the fields to provide for herself and Naomi.

Several things caught the attention of Boaz:

- Her willingness to work hard... (He left her handfuls on purpose)

The Apostle Peter affirms the spiritual power that a wife possesses to bring the grace and presence of God into her home. 1 Peter 3:1-6 (NKJV) "Wives, likewise, be submissive to your own husbands, that even if some do not obey the word, they, without a word, may be won by the conduct of their wives, 2 when they observe your chaste conduct accompanied by fear. 3 Do not let your adornment be merely outward---arranging the hair, wearing gold, or putting on fine apparel--- 4 rather let it be the hidden person of the heart, with the incorruptible beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which is very precious in the sight of God. 5 For in this manner, in former times, the holy women who trusted in God also adorned themselves, being submissive to their own husbands, 6 as Sarah obeyed Abraham, calling him lord, whose daughters you are if you do good and are not afraid with any terror."

We spoke at length last week about the husband's responsibility of leadership in the family. A role each Christian husband is to exercise through sacrificial love.

Peter and Paul both commend to us the importance of a wife showing R-E-S-P-E-C-T to her husband. Paul says, "As unto the Lord."

This does not mean the husband is his wife's god. It means the wife gives respect to her husband as an offering to the Lord.

Peter states that "fear," (respect) coupled with "chaste behavior" has the power to win an unbelieving husband to salvation and by implication, to wield great influence on a godly husband.

Chaste behavior refers to purity of motives and of good moral behavior. It speaks of walking in holiness -- modeling godly character.

Peter carries it further, commending a "gentle" and "quiet" spirit.

- Gentle... The Holman Bible Dictionary notes that this refers to humility. It is not weakness or passivity -- it is controlled power.

- Quiet... Keeping one's seat. Remaining still and at peace.

- Proverbs 21:9 (NKJV) "Better to dwell in a corner of a housetop, Than in a house shared with a contentious woman."
