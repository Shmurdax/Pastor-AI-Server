> WALKING THE FENCE

Recently an elderly minister friend related a story to me out of his
past ministry experience and I would like to share it with you along
with what I believe to be a very practical application for life and
ministry in the 21^st^ Century.

Many years ago my friend spent several years in Scotland doing missions
work. During this time he became personally acquainted with many people
who made their living as shepherds. One day he was helping a shepherd
friend tend to the needs of his flock and the shepherd realized one of
his ewes, who was expected to calve any day, was missing. In an effort
to find the missing ewe the shepherd asked the minister to "Walk the
Fence" because the shepherd had not done so for a couple of days.

Walking the fence is a normal part of shepherding. Walking the fence is
something that is done at least several times a week. When a Shepherd
walks the fence he does so with several things in mind: (PLACE ON SCREEN
ONE AT A TIME)

- He is attempting to discover whether the fence has been
 [breached]{.underline}...

- He is looking to see if anything has entered or exited the property
 that would not be in the [best interest]{.underline} of the sheep...

- He is looking for [hazards]{.underline} that might be detrimental to
 the sheep... (i.e. A fence which is down; a loose fence post; a
 sagging wire; a wire which has been left loose and might get caught in
 the wool of the sheep; etc)

- He is making sure everything inside the fence and in close proximity
 to the fence, in no way [poses]{.underline} a [danger]{.underline} to
 his flock...

Walking the fence might seem at times to be an insignificant part of
shepherding, but in reality it can mean the difference between success
and failure for the shepherd, and it could very well be the difference
between life and death for the sheep.

On this particular day, my friend began his journey around the fence,
and before long he found the ewe and her newborn lamb, both dead, inside
the fence. The ewe had ventured too closely to the barbed wire fence,
which had been repaired a few weeks earlier. The person who had made the
repairs had failed to cut off a small piece of excess wire just a few
inches in length, and the ewe became entrapped when the barbed wire
buried itself in her coat of wool. To no avail, the ewe had obviously
attempted to free herself from the barbed wire. Because of the physical
exhaustion of attempting to free her-self, she had no strength left, and
died during the calving process. Not only did she perish but her little
lamb died as well!

As church leaders we must be constantly walking the fence, checking on
the ones with which God has entrusted us! We cannot afford for the sheep
or lambs we are watching over for the good shepherd to perish on our
watch! Shepherding is serious business. As under-shepherds, we will
eventually be called upon to give an account for everyone God has placed
in our care!

What does walking the fence look like for 21^st^ century church leader?
(PLACE ON SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- We walk the fence by routinely [praying]{.underline} for the people
 who have been entrusted to us... Many times God will reveal certain
 things about certain people as we intercede for them.

- We walk the fence by [interacting]{.underline} with the people... It
 is very difficult to shepherd people we never interact with. As we
 interact with them we often see warning signs of things that should
 not be in their lives.

- We walk the fence by paying attention to who they are
 [hanging]{.underline} [out]{.underline} with... Someone said "You can
 tell a lot about a person by looking at who their friends are!"

- We walk the fence by noticing their habit of [church]{.underline}
 [attendance]{.underline}... A pattern of declining church attendance
 is always a warning sign that something is just not right.

- We walk the fence by noticing changes in [giving]{.underline}
 [patterns]{.underline}... When a person decides to attend a church,
 the last thing to come with them is their money, because they must
 first buy into the vision of the house. The first thing to go when a
 person leaves a church is their money!

As church leaders we have been given the wonderful privilege of
oversight of the flock of God. We need to get our hiking boots on and
not fail to walk the fence!

No pastor can walk the fence alone. The responsibility of the key
leaders is to assist the pastor in noticing what is going on in the
lives of people and taking the appropriate action.

Summary:

The following things must be kept at the forefront for pastors and lay
leaders: (PLACE ON SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- The work of key leaders is [caring]{.underline} for the people...

- Prayer and the ministry of the word are very important but there is
 much more to [shepherding]{.underline}...

- Much effort must be invested into being close enough to the people
 that changes in behavior speak to the leader and an
 [appropriate]{.underline} action can be taken...

- The [pastor]{.underline} needs a team of people who are constantly
 walking the fence for the good of the people...

EFFECTIVE CHURCHES

Listen to what happened when the New Testament Church got the structure
right. Acts 6:7 (NIV) "So the word of God spread. [The number of
disciples in Jerusalem increased rapidly]{.underline}, and a large
number of priests became obedient to the faith."

God uses all kinds of churches: big ones, little ones, and medium sized
ones. It is no more wrong for a church to be small than for a church to
be large.

In ministry I have learned there are actually two unpardonable sins:
[failure]{.underline} and [success]{.underline}. One seems to be as
unforgiveable as the other.

What most of our peers are looking for and wanting from us is
mediocrity. If we allow ourselves to stay confined to the mediocre, we
can find acceptance because it makes everyone else feel more
comfortable. What is the driving force behind this? Jealousy! The
problem is that we are constantly comparing ourselves with one another
and we have been warned in the Word not to do such a thing.

While there is nothing wrong with a church being small, there is
something wrong with a church allowing its mentality and congregation to
accept its [smallness]{.underline}. (LEAVE ON SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE)

I realize that there is a glass ceiling in every community, but I am
convinced most churches have never even been close enough to that
ceiling to see it. The excuse for a church remaining small is often "We
are just in a small community and we are doing what we can." Really?

- How long has it been since every door in the community has been
 knocked?

- How long has it been since every home in the community has received
 some kind of information about the church?

- Does every person in the community know what the church is all about?

- Do we know what the people in our community would be looking for if
 they decided to attend church?

I dare say most churches stay small for a [reason]{.underline}.

I am fully aware the proper measure of success for a church is how many
it [sends]{.underline}, not how many it [keeps]{.underline}. The problem
is, many churches are not doing either.

We actually have churches that do not report one conversion in a year.
Not only are they not seeing people saved, they give nothing to
missions. This means the pastor is not winning souls or giving anything
to missions either! "Monkey see, monkey do!"

The first-century church found a paradigm that would work. They
empowered the pastor and staff and then gave them qualified lay people
who could lead a ministry without constant supervision. The role of the
minister then would be to: (PLACE ON THE SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- [Pray]{.underline}...

- [Prepare]{.underline}...

- [Cast]{.underline} [vision]{.underline}...

- [Train]{.underline}...

- [Delegate]{.underline}...

What was the end result? "The word of God spread. The number of
disciples in Jerusalem increased rapidly, and a large number of priests
became obedient to the faith."

So we must ask ourselves, do we want to be a part of an effective
church? If so we must:

- Properly manage conflict... (PLACE ON SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- Give the pastor the best [available]{.underline} leaders to work
 with...

- Give the pastor leaders who are [compatible]{.underline} with his
 personality and ministry style...

- Release the pastor/staff to [lead]{.underline} the church...

- Get ready, get ready, get ready...

Summary:

The following is a list of practical things a successful pastor/staff
led church must do: (PLACE ON SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- The pastor/staff must be [empowered]{.underline} to lead...

- The pastor/staff must [conceive]{.underline} and [develop]{.underline}
 a God vision for the church...

- The pastor/staff must effectively [communicate]{.underline} the
 [vision]{.underline} to the people... (Suggestion: Conduct a family
 meeting at least once a year on a Sunday morning -- A family meeting
 is where the pastor casts vision for the year and conducts a questions
 and answer session at the conclusion of the meeting)

- The pastor/staff must effectively communicate [decisions]{.underline}
 to the people...

- The pastor must conduct [key]{.underline} [leader]{.underline}
 meetings regularly to keep the key leaders on the same page...

- [Key]{.underline} [leaders]{.underline} should attend as many meetings
 as possible...

- The key leader agenda should be [closed]{.underline} and the pastor
 should comply with this as well...

- The agenda should be sent to the key leaders at least
 [one]{.underline} [week]{.underline} prior to the meeting...

- [Caucusing]{.underline} about agenda items should never be allowed...

GRWOTH BARRIERS

If one studies church growth, it soon becomes apparent that there are
common obstacles to growing healthy, vibrant churches. Let's look at a
few of them.

Failure to create a [culture]{.underline} of invitation (LEAVE ON SCREEN
UNTIL NEXT SLIDE) Most pastors and church leaders desire church growth
but fail to know how to create a culture of invitation. We pray, we
motivate, we emphasize...but members still don't invite their friends to
church. Why is this? 

First of all, most churches fail to create a culture of invitation. If
the church is going to grow, there must be a culture of invitation woven
into the DNA of the church. There are many ways to accomplish this, but
I would like to give you a personal experience. In January of 2009, I
started preaching a series of sermons on our responsibility for inviting
the unchurched. The statistics say that 85% of the unchurched would
attend a service if they were to be invited.

I bought an old time attendance register and placed it on a tripod on
the stage and placed a cover over it. When I got up to preach I unveiled
the attendance register and on it I had placed:

- Invited this week...

- Invited last week...

- Saved this week...

- Saved last week...

Sure enough, no one had been invited to come to church and no one
accepted Christ. I placed zeroes on the board and left it for all to see
as I preached my sermon.

The next Sunday the same thing happened, except now we had four zeroes
on the board. We were the zero church. This went on for weeks until the
people finally realized I was going to do this until they started
inviting people. After a while hundreds were being invited and dozens
per week were coming to Christ. I continued this for more than six
months. Creating a culture of invitation is not easy, but it is doable.
If a church is to grow, a culture of invitation is non-negotiable.

Sometimes the truth is, they're embarrassed to invite anyone. The Sunday
service must be the main event. It does't have to be perfect, but it has
to be the best we can make it. The sermon should be well thought out
from the opening remarks to how the altar service will be conducted. The
music must be very good and plenty of rehearsal time should be the norm.

When we try to create a culture of invitation, we will only be
successful if the service is something our people would not be ashamed
to invite their friends to.

Fearing growth will [change]{.underline} the church (LEAVE ON SCREEN
UNTIL NEXT SLIDE)\
Even though members and attenders may not say this, some of them will
subtly fight growth because they fear that when the congregation gets
larger, they won't know everyone. They also fear that the changes
necessary to grow the church will somehow keep the church from meeting
their needs.

The key is that as the church grows larger, it must grow smaller at the
same time. I know that sounds crazy, but let me explain. One of the most
appealing things about church is that it is a place where one can grow
healthy, long-term relationships. It is true -- the larger a church
grows, the more challenging this becomes. Therefore, the answer to this
problem is to find a way to get everyone into small groups. The Sunday
worship service becomes the large gathering and the small groups are
where the intimate relationships are developed. This is certainly not an
easy thing to do, but it is possible with a little thought and reasearch
and a lot of hard work.

Being too [traditional]{.underline} to [transition]{.underline}

(LEAVE ON SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE)\
Believe it or not, the basis for tradition is success. A tradition is
developed because at some point it was "The Thing" and worked very
effectively. Is it not true that when something works, we repeat it over
and over again?

Unfortunately, most things that work are seasonal. They work for a
season, and as the circumstances change, they are no longer as effective
or they don't work at all.

The church is notorious for holding to traditions that haven't worked in
decades. The problem is that we have a tendency to make a tradition
sacred, and when we do we cannot transition because our tradition has
become a holy thing.

Growing churches are constantly evaluating everything they do. When a
program or ministry has run its course, they retire the tradition and
roll something newer and more effective out in its place.

In growing churches, the process of evaluation is always driven by the
questions:

- Is this effective?

- Why are we doing this?

- Is there a more effective way to accomplish our goals?

>  

Failure to identify a [target]{.underline} audience (LEAVE ON SCREEN
UNTIL NEXT SLIDE)\
As much as we would like to, we cannot appeal to everyone. If a local
radio station played a classical song, followed by a country western
song, followed by a rock song, followed by a rap song, who do you think
their audience would be? Everyone? No one?

Radio stations find a niche because they understand people are attracted
by different styles.

The church is trying to out Hollywood... Hollywood! We seem to think we
can attract the crowd by our well oiled programs and productions. The
truth is we are trying to compete with the world, rather than to reach
the world.

Each church leadership team must decide who God is calling them to
reach, and go after that demographic. When we decide to go after a
certain demographic, we must tailor our style to attract our target
audience. This must be done without kicking the Holy Spirit or His gifts
to the curb.

Never forget, the Holy Spirit will not remain at a church that treats
Him like a crazy cousin that no one wants to talk about. He will not be
confined to a backroom or a special service. No matter what the
demographic is that you are targeting, the Holy Spirit must be front and
center in all that we do.

Failure to value [process]{.underline} above [programs]{.underline}
(PLACE ON THE SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)\
Most plateaued and declining churches have one thing in common. They
believe their programs are going to keep people and grow the church. The
fact is, programs do not grow the church, processes grow the church.

For instance if our vision is to reach, teach and send, we cannot
accomplish this with programming. If we are to be successful in reaching
the stated goals, we must establish an outreach that engages our members
in reaching out to the unchurched. We must also establish small groups
that are effective in discipling people into mature believers. Then we
must have a launching mechanism that sends the disciples out to connect
with the unchurched.

The healthy, vibrant, growing church gets this, and they not only
develop a process to bring the vision to a reality, they also constantly
evaluate all prossesses.

Allowing meetings to [trump]{.underline} ministry (LEAVE ON SCREEN UNTIL
NEXT SLIDE)\
I think we can all agree that you cannot judge a church by how many it
keeps, but by how many it sends. If this is true, why are we so number
conscious? Most churches have one measurement of success -- numbers.
Whether is is attendance numbers at our meetings or budget numbers, this
seems to be the scorecard being used most frequently.

Is God interested in numbers? He named a book in His Bible Numbers!
Numbers are important, but when this is the only measurement of success,
we quickly begin producing passive spectators who are difficult to
motivate beyond attending a meeting to get their nose counted.

What we are trying to produce are disciples. One day when I was praying,
the Lord gave me the following definition of what it means to make a
disciple. "You have not made a disciple until you have reached and
trained an individual, and they have reached someone and are in the
process of discipling that individual."

- The Profit:

- People...

- Processes...

- Product...

Numbers will not guarantee discipleship, but proper processes will.

Lack of [trust]{.underline} among leaders

(LEAVE ON SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE)\
One of the most significant deterents to church growth is lack of trust.
If the top level leaders do not trust each other, nothing else is going
to work right.

When key leaders do not trust each other, it will always follow that the
congregation will find it difficult to trust the leaders. This is
because a lack of trust among key leaders always causes infighting, and
eventually the congregation is brought into the disagreement and the
church starts to implode from the inside.

The vision the pastor is casting in the church must be followed and
implemented by the key leaders. If a key leader cannot agree with the
direction of the pastor, they should do what they can to resolve the
issue. If it cannot be resolved, they should quietly remove themselves
from the leadership team.

Church leaders should always bear in mind that the end result of a lack
of trust is a church fight. Every church fight will bring with it
casulties. I have never seen a church fight that did not end with some
people leaving the church never to return to any church.

This should be a consideration before we decide to allow our staunch
position to allow us to draw the battle lines. Churches decline and die
when they allow their lack of trust to disintegrate into infighting.

[Control]{.underline} issues

(LEAVE ON SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE)\
Church politics strangles the life out of most churches. Many times the
pecking order has been long established and change comes slowly and
painfully if at all.

The problem with unhealthy control in a church is that it is almost
always slanted toward some power brokers. God never intended His church
to operate in this manner. The church is supposed to be a theocricy not
a democracy.

It is true there should be collaboration along the way, but according to
the Word of God the following model develops into a healthy church:
(PLACE ON THE SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- Christ is the [head]{.underline} of the church...

- The pastor is the [undershepherd]{.underline}...

- The key leaders are chosen to serve the [pastor]{.underline} and his
 vision...

- The key leaders are trained by the pastor to do the work of the
 [ministry]{.underline}...

Control issues should never exist in the church; the Word of God gave us
the model for building an effective church and we should be diligent to
follow it implicitly.

Summary:

If we are to remove the barriers to church growth we must: (PLACE ON
SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- Find a way to create a [culture]{.underline} of
 [invitation]{.underline}...

- Discover ways of growing [smaller]{.underline} while growing
 [larger]{.underline}...

- Be aware of what has run its [course]{.underline} and replace it with
 something that works in today's world...

- Know the [demographics]{.underline} of who you are attempting to reach
 and develop a strategy that works with your target audience...

- Set well defined [processes]{.underline} in place for everything you
 are attempting to accomplish...

- [Ministry]{.underline} rather than [meetings]{.underline} must be the
 goal...

- Foster a culture of [trust]{.underline}...

- All [power]{.underline} [brokers]{.underline} must take a step back or
 be dealt with...

TIPPING POINTS

The phrase tipping point means: (PLACE ON THE SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- The moment of [critical mass]{.underline}...

- The [boiling]{.underline} point...

- The moment when something [unique]{.underline} becomes
 [common]{.underline}...

- The dramatic moment in an [organization]{.underline} when everything
 changes at once...

If we take a moment or two, we can all think of tipping points along the
pathway of life:

- Conversion...

- Graduation...

- Marriage...

- Buying a home...

- Having a baby...

- Facing health issues...

- Retirement...

A church reaches tipping points as well. Sadly, some tipping points are
[detrimental]{.underline} while many are [high water marks]{.underline}
in the life of the church. (LEAVE ON SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE) We have
all lived through a plethora of both.

Healthy change is normally preceded with grueling, sometimes exhausting
incremental steps of thought and action, but the fact remains that
tipping points occur in one dramatic moment. We must be sensitive to the
timing and the people among whom we minister, but we must also be aware
when a tipping point is at hand. This is called context. When a tipping
point transpires the expectation level rises.

The thing about a tipping point in a church is that the expectation
level of the body changes in one [critical]{.underline} moment. (LEAVE
ON THE SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE) The old adage is that when the tide
rises, the level of all the boats in the harbor changes.

When an organization achieves a level of success with an event or the
spiritual mood reaches a crescendo, everything we do from that day
forward is judged against the backdrop of the success of that moment, as
it should be.

In thinking through our many years of ministry at CT Church, there have
been many tipping points:

- The first Easter outreach...

- The first Health Fair...

- Confronting a bully serving on the Board...

- Planting churches...

- Opening additional campuses and venues...

- Take the Plunge Sunday...

- Red Bandana Sunday...

- Managing seven Sunday morning services in four locations...

I could go on, but you get the idea that when we reach these critical
tipping points, there is usually no going back whether we are headed in
a good or bad direction.

If our attitude becomes "Then we need to be careful about how high we
raise the bar because we will paint ourselves into a corner" we need
to: (PLACE ON THE SCREEN ONE AT A TIME)

- Get alone with God for a [faith lift]{.underline}...

- Embark on a plan of [personal improvement]{.underline}...

- Decide to stop [impeding]{.underline} the progress of the team...

At every tipping point the expectation level increases for every team
member. Because of this, some team members are only on the team for a
season; others remain throughout the entire journey.

The responsibility level rises. When a tipping point occurs within the
context of the church, everyone on the leadership team must assume new
responsibilities.

If I ever backslide, it will be while driving. The most annoying people
on the earth have a steering wheel in their hands! Slow traffic should
always keep right! Slow drivers in the left lane are inconsiderate,
inexcusable and downright annoying. I have a disdain for people who
drive slow in the left lane.

As a member of the leadership team of a church that is rapidly
transitioning from tipping point to tipping point, every member of the
team will have to decide whether they are going to [impede
traffic]{.underline} or [accelerate their serve]{.underline} to help set
the pace. (LEAVE ON SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE)

As the church cycles through tipping points the level of responsibility
becomes greater for all leaders at all levels. This finds its expression
in:

- Loyalty...

- Faithfulness...

- Productivity...

Team members must always bear in mind that [persuasion]{.underline}
occurs in ways we do not understand or appreciate. (LEAVE ON THE SCREEN
UNTIL NEXT SLIDE)

As leaders, we have a responsibility to make sure our loyalty,
faithfulness and productivity is creating an opportunity for the team to
reach another tipping point.

By our influence, we draw others into our rhythms by our attitudes and
interaction. The people will respond to change in the same way team
members embrace it.

Are we living up to our responsibility of being a team member of a
church transitioning through a series of tipping points?

This church will never be stronger than the weakest link of the
leadership team. We have to ask ourselves -- are we pulling our weight
or are we impeding the progress of the team because we can't stand the
pull?

The impact level rises. The really interesting thing about tipping
points is that in the beginning, the progress realized from tipping
points is minuet, almost immeasurable. As the organization passes the
mileposts of one tipping point after another, the impact becomes more
and more measureable.

Finally one discovers the tipping points are extremely significant
because yesterday's [ceiling]{.underline} is today's
[floor]{.underline}. (LEAVE ON THE SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE) The
trajectory of the organization can be charted on a graph where there are
few plateaus or downturns, and the trip becomes one gradual climb.

In such a scenario, each team member MUST realize that any deviation
from 100% effort or uniformity can result in a loss of impact and the
growth of the organization is adversely affected. Loss of momentum is
the death rattle of the organization. Think of this:

- A bad report from ten spies kept each of the ten out of the Promised
 Land for a lifetime...

- A bad report from ten spies kept an entire generation out of the
 Promised Land forever...

- A bad report from ten spies kept their own families out of the
 Promised Land for more than forty years...

- A bad report from ten spies kept two men of faith from the Promised
 Land for more than forty years...

The loss of impact in this scenario is significant. The last thing we
want or need is a loss of impact.

Small [actions]{.underline} can have [momentous]{.underline} effects.
(LEAVE ON THE SCREEN UNTIL NEXT SLIDE) The truth is that small things
matter as much as big things. In golf it means nothing to be able to
drive the ball three hundred yards if ones chipping and putting are a
disaster.

A church leadership team must always be on a quest to find new ways to
create tipping points. With the help of the Holy Spirit and the
creativity of a team, tipping points can become more significant and
more frequent as the team moves forward.

A team working together in harmony produces a synergy that creates a
culture of [maximum impact]{.underline}. (LEAVE ON SCREEN UNTIL NEXT
SLIDE) Leviticus 26:8 (NKJV) "Five of you shall chase a hundred, and
a hundred of you shall put ten thousand to flight; your enemies shall
fall by the sword before you." If one extrapolates the effect created
by "Five chasing a hundred" it stands to reason that one hundred
can chase two thousand. Yet the Bible says "A hundred of you can put
ten thousand to flight!"

Because of the synergy of a team, the ability of a person to produce
rises from a ratio of one-to-twenty to one-to-one hundred!

A culture of tipping points can be created by: (PLACE ON THE SCREEN ONE
AT A TIME)

- [Evaluating]{.underline} everything...

- Stopping the things that are not [working]{.underline}...

- Making [improvements]{.underline} to the things that are working...

- Making changes [sooner]{.underline} rather than [later]{.underline}...

- Always keeping the [objective]{.underline} of the team at the
 forefront of everything...

Summary:

If a team is attempting to create tipping points, they should be asking
the following questions on a regular basis: (PLACE ON THE SCREEN ONE AT
A TIME)

- Have we [missed]{.underline} a tipping point? Why?

- Are we [currently]{.underline} at a tipping point?

- How can we [create]{.underline} a tipping point?

- How can we [maximize]{.underline} the moment of the next tipping
 point?
